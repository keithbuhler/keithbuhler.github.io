That Which Is Deeply Good

An Introduction to Classical Christian Education

The Question of Attention

  • Not just in the modern world, but for all time, our life is pulled by distractions. The most fundamental question for education is: What is truly worth our time and attention?
  • Education is not just about preparing students for jobs; it’s forming their souls by focusing their attention for 10,000 hours—the time spent in the classroom for 12 of the most impressionable years of their lives.

The Wisdom of the Ages

  • Classical educators love old things: old books, old music, old ideas.
  • This puts classical education at odds with modernity, which often sees the old as outdated or irrelevant.
  • The "Lindy Effect": The longer something has lasted, the longer it is likely to last. Things that endure have proven their value.

Spurgeon's Law: Most everything is not good.

  • A critical distinction: "Not everything old is good, and not everything good is old." We value things because they are old *and* have been consistently identified as deeply good.
  • Some things have a quality — let’s call it “deep goodness” — that is rare, precious, and hard to identify.
    (Gilead by Marilynne Robinson, Partita for 8 Voices by Caroline Shaw)

The Test of Time

  • Time is a great filter. Most bad old things have been forgotten. Most good old things have stuck around.
  • Examples: Sacred Scripture!
  • The works of Homer, Virgil, and Dante
  • The architecture and sculpture of the Greeks, Romans, Byzantines.
  • The films of Chaplin, Tarkovsky
  • Recent works such as Tolkien, Lewis, Sherlock Holmes... still 'on trial' but they can be 'instant classics.'

Learning to Love the Good

  • A challenge: That which is deeply good is often hard to identify, and hard to love at first.
  • To identify: time; to love: contemplation
  • Think of acquiring a taste for black coffee, fine wine, or complex classical music. The initial effort leads to a deeper joy.
  • Virtue is 'ordered love' (ordo amoris)

On Ordered Love

'Can you be righteous', asks Traherne, 'unless you be just in rendering to things their due esteem? All things were made to be yours and you were made to prize them according to their value.' St Augustine defines virtue as ordo amoris, the ordinate condition of the affections in which every object is accorded that kind of degree of love which is appropriate to it. Aristotle says that the aim of education is to make the pupil like and dislike what he ought.

When the age for reflective thought comes, the pupil who has been thus trained in 'ordinate affections' or 'just sentiments' will easily find the first principles in Ethics; but to the corrupt man they will never be visible at all and he can make no progress in that science. Plato before him had said the same. The little human animal will not at first have the right responses. It must be trained to feel pleasure, liking, disgust, and hatred at those things which really are pleasant, likeable, disgusting and hateful.

In the Republic, the well-nurtured youth is one 'who would see most clearly whatever was amiss in ill-made works of man or ill-grown works of nature, and with a just distaste would blame and hate the ugly even from his earliest years and would give delighted praise to beauty, receiving it into his soul and being nourished by it, so that he becomes a man of gentle heart... when Reason at length comes to him... he will hold out his hands in welcome and recognize her because of the affinity he bears to her.'

- C.S. Lewis, The Abolition of Man

Deeply good relationships

  • This principle applies to relationships, too. Marriage and lifelong friendships are harder to cultivate than shallow acquaintances, but the reward is a profound and lasting bond that shallow friendships cannot offer.

Repetition & The Well-Trained Palate

  • How do we learn to love what is difficult but good?
  • Humility requires we admit *don't* yet love the deeply good (at least not enough)
  • Humble, patient repetition. We read the great books again and again.
  • "Staring, soaking" (contemplation) The water contemplates the coffee bean
  • Through this discipline, our tastes are changed and our affections are ordered toward the good.
  • Anecdotess: Duke Ellington, Beethoven, green smoothies.

The Mark of a Reader

"...the majority never read anything twice... Those who read great works... will read the same work ten, twenty or thirty times during the course of their life."

"...literary people are always looking for leisure and silence in which to read and do so with their whole attention... they feel impoverished."

"The first reading of some literary work is often... an experience so momentous... Their whole consciousness is changed. They have become what they were not before."

- C.S. Lewis

Enduring vs. Ephemeral

"Josh Gibbs: qualities [such as catchy, slick, fun] are easy to enjoy because they appeal primarily to the senses, which means that their appeal is immediate and doesn’t ask much of us... Our interest in things which have lasted tends to be lower, and yet it never goes away. This year, Beyoncé will outsell Beethoven. This century, Beethoven will outsell Beyoncé."
"Such This is because the same qualities which make an artist the most popular this year also guarantee their unpopularity in fifty years. The sort of music which lasts is not catchy, clever, sexy, spectacular, shocking, or slick—at least, these are not words most people would use to describe the music of Brahms, Haydn, or Mozart."

How It All Fits Together

  • Basic skills—math, language, history, grammar—are foundational. But they aren’t “the point.” Neither is “getting a job” the point!
  • These skills develop the mind so that it is able to appreciate the deeply good. This is a practical necessity. Being a good employee, parent, and member of society requires that we love what is truly good.

Classical and *Christian*

  • If we read the Bible, we can't be caught up too much in modern fads. It is old and deeply good!
  • We read the Church Fathers out of a love for Truth, not simply a love for the old. We read Scripture and the Fathers because they are deeply good!

Forming the Soul

  • At a classical Christian school, we work on purifying the passions and illuminating the mind and heart through prayer, fasting, confession, and forgiveness.
  • This is why a classical education is so well integrated with the life of the Church: Sunday worship, daily prayers, and book learning.

A Unified Worldview

  • The final goal is to see and understand all things in relation to Christ. History, literature, mathematics, science, and art are not separate subjects, but different facets of God's unified, created reality.

A Summary: Why Classical Christian Education?

  • It humbles us to trust in time-tested wisdom.
  • It gives us access to that which is deeply good, forming our love in the present and expanding our joy.
  • It provides a stable vantage point from which to judge the fleeting ideas of the present.
  • Along with life in the Church, it grounds all learning in Christ, “in whom are all things”.
